Want to learn more?

Fast fashion is a business model that mass-produces trendy clothing at extremely low costs. New styles arrive weekly, encouraging consumers to buy more and discard quickly. Major digital brands add an average of 2,000–6,000 individual styles to their app each day (peaking up to 10,000 during specific periods).

The global fashion industry produces around 100 billion garments annually — roughly 12–13 items for every person on Earth. Studies estimate that 20–30% of this production goes unsold. An estimated 92 million tonnes of textiles end up in landfills each year.

An estimated 75 million people work in the global fashion supply chain, many earning below living wages. The 2013 Rana Plaza factory collapse in Bangladesh killed 1,134 workers, exposing the industry's unsafe conditions. Child labour remains prevalent in cotton farming and garment production.

Many brands use vague terms like "conscious," "eco-friendly," and "sustainable collection" without accountability. Studies show that up to 60% of sustainability claims by fashion brands are misleading. Look for third-party certifications like GOTS, Fair Trade, or B-Corp.

1. Raw Materials

Cotton accounts for roughly 2.4% of the world's cultivated land but uses an estimated 4.7% to 6% of the world's pesticides and 16% of insecticides. Synthetic fibres like polyester are derived from fossil fuels and take 200+ years to decompose.

2. Manufacturing

Textile dyeing uses approximately 1.5 trillion litres of water annually. Chemical runoff from factories pollutes rivers and groundwater in producing countries.

3. Transportation

A garment may travel 20,000+ km from raw material to your wardrobe — from cotton fields in India, to factories in Bangladesh, to shops in Europe or America.

4. Use (Average: 7 wears)

The average fast fashion garment is worn just 7 times (an illustrative estimate based on a Barnardo's UK report). Each wash of synthetic clothing releases up to 700,000 microplastic fibres into water systems.

5. Disposal

Less than 1% of clothing is recycled into new garments. Most ends up in landfills, incinerators, or exported to developing nations where it overwhelms local markets and ecosystems.
